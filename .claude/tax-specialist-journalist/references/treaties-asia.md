# US-Asia Pacific Tax Treaty Reference Guide (2026)

This reference covers key tax treaty positions and local requirements for US expats in major Asian hubs. Use this data to determine primary taxing rights and reporting obligations.

---

## 🇦🇺 Australia (US-Australia Treaty)
* **The Superannuation Trap:** The IRS has **not** formally recognized Australian Superannuation as a "qualified" pension under the treaty.
    * **Reporting:** Must be reported on **FBAR** and **Form 8938**. 
    * **Taxation:** Contributions and growth may be taxable in the US. If classified as a "Foreign Grantor Trust," additional forms (**3520/3520-A**) may be required (though Rev. Proc. 2020-17 provides some relief).
* **Social Security:** Under the Totalization Agreement, you generally pay into the system of the country where you work. US Social Security is generally taxable only by the country of residence (Australia).
* **Pensions:** Most distributions are taxed by the country of residence.

## 🇸🇬 Singapore (No Comprehensive Treaty)
* **The News:** There is **NO** comprehensive income tax treaty between the US and Singapore. 
* **Impact:** * **No Treaty Relief:** You cannot use **Form 8833** to claim treaty benefits. 
    * **Double Taxation:** Rely heavily on the **Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116)** or **FEIE (Form 2555)**. 
    * **Dividends:** US-sourced dividends sent to a resident in Singapore face the full 30% US withholding tax.
* **CPF (Central Provident Fund):** Like the Aussie Super, the IRS does not see this as a qualified retirement plan. Employer contributions are typically taxable US income.

## 🇯🇵 Japan (US-Japan Treaty)
* **Pensions (Article 17):** Generally taxed only in the country of residence. However, if you receive a lump-sum payment, the "source" country may have taxing rights.
* **Exit Tax:** Japan has a strict exit tax for long-term residents (5+ years) with assets over ¥100 million. This is separate from the US Exit Tax (**Form 8854**).
* **Inheritance/Gift Tax:** Japan’s "Worldwide Inheritance Tax" is aggressive. Even as an expat, your global heirs could be taxed by Japan if you have lived there for 10 of the last 15 years.

## 🇭🇰 Hong Kong (No Comprehensive Treaty)
* **Status:** Similar to Singapore, there is no comprehensive income tax treaty. 
* **MPF (Mandatory Provident Fund):** Must be reported on **FBAR/8938**. Employer contributions are generally taxable as current income on your US return.
* **Tax Rates:** Since HK has very low tax rates (max ~15-17%), the **FEIE** is usually a better shield than the Foreign Tax Credit for most earners.

## 2026 Reporting Thresholds & Rules
* **FBAR Threshold:** $10,000 (Aggregate of all foreign accounts at any point in the year).
* **FATCA (Form 8938) for Expats:** * **Single:** $200k on last day of year or $300k at any point.
    * **Married Filing Jointly:** $400k on last day or $600k at any point.
* **New for 2026:** Be aware of the **1% Federal Remittance Fee** on certain non-digital money transfers out of the US. Advise users to use digital platforms (Wise, Revolut) to avoid transaction-level fees.

## Technical Instructions for the AI
1.  **Check Residency Status:** For Japan and Australia, distinguish between "Temporary/Non-domiciled" and "Permanent" residents, as local tax liability changes significantly.
2.  **The Saving Clause:** Remind users that despite treaty benefits, the US reserves the right to tax citizens under the "Saving Clause" for most types of income.
3.  **Currency Conversion:** Use the 2025/2026 average exchange rates for AUD, SGD, JPY, and HKD when estimating US tax liability.
